Learning Science

Smart Planner is inspired by simple, evidence-informed study habits: space it out, recall first, focus in clear blocks, and write a short summary in your own words.

This page explains the learning ideas behind Smart Planner. It is not medical, educational, or exam-result advice. The app helps you build better study habits, but it does not guarantee memory or grade outcomes.

1. Spaced review: less cramming, more return

Many learners review too much at once and then forget to come back. Smart Planner uses loops so important material returns later. This is based on the general idea of distributed practice: spreading learning across time instead of relying only on one long session.

Smart Planner turns this into a practical action: create a loop, choose a repeat pattern, and let the reminder bring the material back.

2. Active recall: remember before rereading

Review is stronger when it is active. Instead of immediately rereading, pause and try to remember what you learned. This is why Smart Planner phrases review as a loop, not just another reading task.

A good review flow is simple: see the reminder, close the source for a moment, recall what you can, then check and correct.

3. Focus timer: a practical rhythm, not a magic number

Smart Planner includes a focus timer because starting is often the hardest part. A timer gives the study session a clear boundary and reduces friction.

The app does not claim that one session length is perfect for everyone. A 25-minute block is a practical default, while some users may prefer 30, 45, or 50 minutes depending on the task and energy level.

4. Recall plus short writing

After a focus session or reminder, writing a short summary in your own words can make review more active. The point is not to create long notes. The point is to process the idea again and make it easier to return to later.

Smart Planner encourages short review notes because a small written summary is easier to repeat than a heavy study log.

5. How Smart Planner applies this

  • Spaced Loops: bring study material back over time.
  • Focus Timer: help you start and finish a focused block.
  • Review reminders: prompt recall before rereading.
  • Short notes: help you summarize what matters in your own words.
  • Local-first design: keep the core planner simple and private on your device in v1.

References

  • Roediger & Karpicke (2006) - research on test-enhanced learning and delayed retention.
  • Mueller & Oppenheimer (2014) - research on longhand note taking and deeper processing.
